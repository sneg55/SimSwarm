STRAIT OF HORMUZ CRISIS — March 2026

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on March 20, 2026, turning away three container ships. Brent crude oil surpassed $110 per barrel within hours.

Key developments (March 20-27):
- US President Trump issued a 48-hour ultimatum for Iran to reopen the strait or face strikes on Iranian power stations. The deadline was later extended by 10 days to April 6.
- Iran stated that "non-hostile" vessels may pass if they comply with Iranian safety requirements, excluding ships linked to countries involved in the war.
- COSCO Shipping resumed container bookings from Far East to Gulf countries after Iran allowed select vessels through.
- The US Treasury temporarily lifted sanctions on Iranian oil at sea for 30 days to ease supply pressure.
- The US Department of Energy awarded contracts to loan 45.2 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve.
- Norway cut petrol and diesel taxes. Slovenia introduced fuel rationing — the first EU country to do so. South Sudan and Mauritius imposed electricity consumption restrictions. The Philippines declared a national energy emergency.
- QatarEnergy declared force majeure on some long-term LNG contracts.
- Philippine Airlines halted Middle East flights until April 30.
- Gold prices in Dubai rose after a 15-day drop amid potential diplomacy.
- Brent crude fell 11% to below $100/barrel after Trump announced "productive conversations" with Iran, then rebounded above $110 when negotiations stalled.

The G7 foreign ministers reached an agreement to protect passage through the Strait if the war ends. Iran's parliament began preparing legislation to impose fees on ships for "safe passage."

Approximately 21% of the world's daily oil consumption passes through the Strait of Hormuz.

JURY RULES AGAINST GOOGLE AND META IN CHILD ADDICTION CASE — March 25, 2026

Case: K.G.M. v. Meta et al.

A jury in California ruled in favor of a woman who sued Google and Meta Platforms for her addictions to Instagram and YouTube as a child. The landmark verdict establishes that social media platforms can be held liable for addictive design features targeting minors.

Case details:
- The plaintiff (identified as K.G.M.) alleged she became addicted to Instagram and YouTube during childhood, causing psychological harm.
- The suit targeted specific platform design features: infinite scroll, autoplay, notification systems, and algorithmic content recommendations optimized for engagement.
- The jury found both Google (YouTube) and Meta (Instagram) liable.
- Damages and remedies have not yet been announced.

Legal context:
- This is the first successful jury verdict holding social media companies liable for addictive design targeting children.
- Hundreds of similar cases are pending in US federal and state courts, consolidated under MDL (multidistrict litigation).
- The US Surgeon General has called social media a "public health crisis" for youth mental health.
- COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act) currently regulates data collection from children under 13 but does not address addictive design.
- Several US states have passed or proposed age verification laws for social media.
- The EU's Digital Services Act includes provisions on protecting minors online.

Industry implications:
- Meta and Google are expected to appeal. The case could reach the Supreme Court on First Amendment grounds.
- Platform stocks may face pressure as the ruling opens the door to massive class action liability.
- Other platforms (TikTok, Snapchat, X) face similar design liability exposure.
- The ruling could accelerate legislative action on children's online safety at federal and state levels.
- Platform companies may preemptively redesign features for minors to reduce litigation risk.
